# botanical-illustration

Precise scientific illustration of plants rendered on cream-tinted paper — delicate watercolor washes over meticulous graphite linework, cross-sections revealing internal structures, seeds and roots arranged with taxonomic care, handwritten labels in elegant copperplate script. This is the tradition of Maria Sibylla Merian and Pierre-Joseph Redoute: observation elevated to art, every vein and petal rendered with scientific devotion and aesthetic grace.

## Variants

| Variant | Focus | Visual Emphasis |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Specimen Plate** | Complete plant documentation, all parts shown | Full plant from root to flower arranged on page — main stem vertical at center, detached details (leaf close-up, cross-section of stem, individual seeds) arranged around margins with fine ruled lines connecting to source |
| **Herbarium** | Pressed and dried plant appearance, flat and preserved | Flattened plant form as if pressed between pages, visible tape or mounting corners, handwritten collection data (date, location), slightly faded and aged colors |
| **Cross-Section** | Internal anatomy revealed, educational dissection | Clean surgical cuts through stem, fruit, flower showing internal chambers and structures — each section labeled with thin ruled lines and numbered callouts |
| **Life Cycle** | Seed to sprout to flower to seed, developmental progression | Horizontal arrangement from left to right showing growth stages — seed, germination, seedling, mature plant, flower, fruit, seed release |

## Visual Elements

- Fine graphite or ink linework defining edges, veins, and textures with hairline precision
- Subtle watercolor washes layered from light to dark, allowing paper tone to glow through
- Visible paper texture — cream or ivory background is integral to the aesthetic
- Individual leaf veins traced with scientific accuracy, branching patterns clearly visible
- Cross-section views with cleanly cut edges, internal chambers and structures exposed
- Seeds, pollen, and microscopic details shown enlarged in corner insets
- Thin ruled lines (leader lines) connecting detail views to their source on main illustration
- Handwritten labels in copperplate or italic script, sometimes in Latin
- Scale bars or measurement notations (e.g., "x 2" for magnification)
- Roots rendered in earth tones, often showing soil line as clear horizontal boundary
- Dewdrops and subtle shine on surfaces rendered with white paper showing through
- Shadows kept minimal and delicate — soft gray suggesting depth without drama
- Occasional pressed-flower effect: flattened, slightly asymmetrical, preserved
- Aged paper effects: subtle foxing (brown spots), soft edges, faint discoloration

| Comparison | Two or more specimens side by side with parallel labeling systems | Comparative plate showing related species |
| Categories | Grid of individual specimens, each in its own bounded area with labels | Herbarium sheet with multiple pressed samples |

## Typography

- Primary: Elegant serif with old-style figures — Garamond, Caslon, or Baskerville
- Labels: Copperplate script or italic hand for species names, particularly Latin binomials
- Title in classical capitals with subtle letter-spacing, often at top of plate
- Numbered callouts in small, refined figures (1, 2, 3) with corresponding legend
- Scale notations in fine italic: "nat. size" or "x 2" or "1 cm"
- All text in sepia brown or india ink black, never colored
- Generous margins and careful text placement — never crowded or overlapping illustration
- Scientific names in italics: *Genus species*
- Hand-lettered appearance preferred over digital fonts where possible

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT bright saturated colors** — Botanical illustration uses muted, naturalistic color achieved through watercolor washes. No neon, no pure saturated hues, no digital color.
- **NOT photorealistic** — This is illustration, not photography. Lines are drawn, colors are washed, paper shows through. No photographic textures or 3D rendering.
- **NOT modern minimalist** — Traditional botanical illustration is richly detailed. Empty space should be cream paper, not stark white. Labels and details fill the margins.
- **NOT cartoon or stylized** — Shapes should be observed and accurate, not simplified or exaggerated. Every curve follows botanical reality.
- **NOT dark or dramatic** — Lighting is even and observational, like a specimen under studio lights. No dramatic shadows, no mood lighting, no chiaroscuro.
- **NOT cluttered** — Despite richness of detail, composition should be organized and elegant. Clear hierarchy, breathing room around specimens, logical label placement.
- **NOT digital vector art** — Must feel hand-drawn and hand-painted. Visible pencil texture, watercolor edge variation, subtle imperfections.
- This must look like a plate from Curtis's Botanical Magazine or Redoute's Les Roses — meticulous, scientific, elegantly labeled, on warm cream paper — not a modern graphic design using plant imagery.
