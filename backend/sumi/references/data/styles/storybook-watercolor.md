# storybook-watercolor

Soft translucent watercolor washes bleeding gently past pencil outlines on thick cream paper — a cozy cottage with a curl of chimney smoke, a rabbit in a blue coat standing in a doorway, wildflowers dotting a meadow rendered in loose wet-on-wet puddles of lavender and buttercup yellow. Every edge bleeds softly, every shadow is a transparent violet wash layered over warm underpainting, and the white paper glows through every color like sunlight through stained glass. This is Beatrix Potter's Lake District, Ernest Shepard's Hundred Acre Wood, a bedtime story painted in tea-stained watercolor on paper that smells like old libraries.

## Color Palette

- Primary: Dusty cornflower blue (#7BA7CC), warm honey gold (#E8C872), soft sage green (#8DB580), muted rose pink (#D4899A)
- Background: Warm cream paper (#FBF5E8) showing through every wash as luminous ground, soft sky wash (#D6E4F0) for exterior scenes
- Darks: Warm sepia brown (#7A5C42) for outlines and tree trunks, soft charcoal (#5A5A5A) for text — never harsh black
- Accents: Buttercup yellow (#F0D060), lavender (#B8A0C8), robin-egg blue (#89C4D4), terracotta (#C47A5A), moss green (#6B8F5A), berry red (#B8475A)
- Text: Warm sepia (#7A5C42) or soft charcoal (#5A5A5A) on cream — always warm, never cold black

## Visual Elements

- **Transparent watercolor washes that let cream paper glow through** — every color is TRANSLUCENT, never opaque. The warm paper luminosity shines through blue skies, green meadows, and pink roses equally. Colors mix on the paper surface where washes overlap, creating organic color transitions
- **Soft bleeding edges where watercolor escapes pencil lines** — color washes bleed slightly past their pencil-drawn boundaries, creating a gentle halo effect. Water pools at edges forming darker rims (cauliflower edges) that add organic texture. Nothing is crisply contained
- **Delicate pencil or sepia ink outlines under watercolor** — fine, slightly wobbly pencil or pen lines drawn first, then watercolor washes applied loosely on top. The lines are visible beneath the color, warm and organic, not mechanical. Variable line weight — heavier for near objects, lighter for distant
- **Anthropomorphic animals in lovingly detailed period clothing** — rabbits in blue jackets with brass buttons, mice in aprons and bonnets, badgers in waistcoats, hedgehogs in shawls. Animals stand upright with human gestures but retain animal proportions and fur texture
- **Cozy cottage architecture** — stone cottages with thatched or slate roofs, curl of chimney smoke, climbing roses on trellised walls, small-paned windows glowing warm yellow from within, wooden garden gates, moss on roof tiles
- **Wildflower meadows rendered in loose wet-on-wet technique** — individual flower heads as small dots and dabs of diluted color (lavender, buttercup, rose) scattered across green washes, some colors bleeding into each other, the meadow as an impressionistic color field
- **Visible paper texture interacting with watercolor** — the grain of thick watercolor paper catching pigment in its valleys, creating a granulated texture especially visible in sky washes and shadow areas. The paper is a character, not just a surface
- **Warm golden light** — scenes lit as if by late afternoon sun or candlelight, casting long soft shadows in transparent violet or blue-grey washes layered over the warm ground colors
- **Botanical precision for key plants and flowers** — important plants rendered with careful watercolor observation: individual petals with veining, leaves with serrated edges, stems with thorns. The natural world treated with loving attention
- **Cozy interior details** — kitchen shelves with labeled jars, a kettle on a stove, checkered tablecloth, a rocking chair by a fire, books stacked on a window seat, dried herbs hanging from rafters. Every interior tells a story of domestic life
- **Soft atmospheric perspective** — distant hills rendered as simple pale blue-grey washes, midground in muted greens, foreground with full color saturation and detail. Distance = paleness and simplification
- **Small background creatures and details** — a robin on a fence post, a ladybug on a leaf, a spider web catching morning dew, a snail on a stone wall — tiny discoveries that reward close looking
- **NO harsh black outlines, NO flat digital color fills, NO airbrushed gradients** — everything is hand-painted watercolor on paper with visible brush marks, water blooms, and pigment granulation

## Typography

- Warm sepia or soft charcoal hand-lettered text — slightly irregular letter shapes as if written with a dip pen, gentle and readable
- Serif book typeface feel for body text — reminiscent of classic children's book typesetting, comfortable and familiar like an old library book
- Titles in gentle hand-lettered calligraphy with watercolor flourishes — a small painted flower or leaf integrated into the first letter
- Labels in small neat handwriting alongside sepia leader lines — like annotations in a naturalist's field journal
- Text placed in cream panels or on unpainted paper areas, never overlaying watercolor illustrations — text and image are neighbors, not layers
- Generous margins and line spacing — the page should breathe, never feel cramped
- NO bold sans-serif, NO all-caps shouting, NO digital precision — everything is warm, gentle, hand-crafted, and inviting to read aloud
- Page numbers in small decorative frames with tiny botanical motifs

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT Studio Ghibli** — Ghibli is animation-quality gouache with smooth graduated backgrounds and cinematic framing. Storybook watercolor is TRANSPARENT watercolor on TEXTURED PAPER with visible water blooms, pigment granulation, and bleeding edges. If the color is opaque and the surface is smooth, it's Ghibli, not storybook.
- **NOT sumi-e or shan-shui** — Those are INK on paper, monochrome, zen/philosophical. Storybook watercolor is COLORED, warm, narrative, cozy. If it's black-and-white with misty mountains, it's the wrong continent and the wrong century.
- **NOT Richard Scarry** — Scarry is flat, primary-colored, label-dense busy scenes. Storybook watercolor is soft, translucent, atmospheric, gentle. If the scene is crammed with 20 labeled characters, it's Scarry. If it's a quiet cottage with a single rabbit, it's storybook.
- **NOT botanical-illustration** — Botanical illustration is scientific, labeled, clinical precision on white paper. Storybook watercolor uses botanical SKILL in service of NARRATIVE warmth. If it looks like a museum specimen plate, it's too clinical.
- **NOT digital watercolor filter** — Real watercolor has UNPREDICTABLE water behavior: cauliflower edges, pigment granulation, uneven washes, happy accidents. If the washes are perfectly smooth and uniform, it's a Photoshop filter, not watercolor.
- **NOT bright or saturated** — all colors are DILUTED and TRANSPARENT. If the blue is electric, the red is fire-engine, or the green is neon, it's far too intense. Every color should look like it was mixed with a lot of water.
- **NOT dark or moody** — even night scenes retain warmth through candlelight yellows and warm sepia. This is a COMFORTING aesthetic. If it feels melancholy, spooky, or noir, it's wrong.
- **NOT minimal or empty** — storybook scenes are filled with cozy detail: shelf contents, garden plants, fabric patterns, food on tables. But the detail is LOVING and ORGANIC, not clinical or overwhelming.
- This must look like a page torn from a beloved children's book that's been read so many times the spine is cracked and the pages are soft — transparent watercolor on thick cream paper, sepia pen lines, animals in waistcoats, cottage gardens, and the warm glow of a story being told at bedtime.
