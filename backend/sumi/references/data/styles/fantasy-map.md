# fantasy-map

Aged parchment unfurled to reveal a hand-inked cartographer's masterwork — mountain ranges rendered as clusters of tiny peaked triangles, forests as dense fields of lollipop-shaped trees, coastlines traced with rippling wave lines, and every landmark labeled in elegant calligraphic script with flourishing serifs. This is Tolkien's Middle-earth, a medieval manuscript margin, a treasure map from a leather-bound adventure novel — ancient, hand-crafted, and inviting exploration.

## Color Palette

- Primary: Sepia ink (#5D4037), aged parchment cream (#F4E4BC), forest green (#3E5F4E)
- Background: Weathered parchment (#F4E4BC) with subtle staining, tea-stained edges (#D4B896), antique vellum (#EDE0C8)
- Darks: Dark sepia (#3E2723), charcoal ink (#2D2D2D) — for outlines, text, and detailed linework
- Accents: Ocean blue (#5B8FA8), mountain slate gray (#6B7B8C), deep burgundy red (#8B3A3A for X-marks and borders), gold leaf (#C9A227 for special elements)
- Text: Dark sepia on parchment; cream/gold on darker decorative cartouches; always warm-toned, never cold black

## Visual Elements

- **Mountains as clustered peaked triangles** — ranges rendered as rows of small ^^ shapes with internal hatching lines suggesting ridges, casting shadows to one consistent side
- **Forests as tree clusters** — dense fields of tiny lollipop-shaped trees (circle on a stick) packed together, varying slightly in size, with occasional individual trees at forest edges
- **Coastlines with wave ripples** — land edges traced with wavy parallel lines suggesting water, waves getting smaller further from shore, sea areas filled with gentle ripple patterns
- **Rivers as double-lined paths** — waterways drawn with two parallel wavy lines, widening toward the sea, with tributaries branching off
- **Settlements as tiny building clusters** — towns shown as clusters of small pitched-roof rectangles, cities with more buildings and a central tower or castle, villages as just 2-3 houses
- **Compass rose** — ornate multi-pointed star indicating North, with decorative fletching on the cardinal directions, often placed in ocean areas
- **Decorative cartouches** — titles and legends enclosed in scrollwork borders, like unfurled ribbons or baroque frames with curling edges
- **Dotted paths and routes** — travel routes marked with dashed lines, sometimes with small footprint marks or arrow heads
- **Sea monsters and ships** — ocean areas populated with small sailing vessels and stylized sea creatures (serpents, whales, kraken tentacles)
- **Aged parchment texture** — visible paper grain, subtle staining, darker edges suggesting age and handling, occasional fold lines
- **Hatching and stippling** — shading achieved through parallel lines or dots, never gradients — everything rendered in pen-and-ink technique
- **Ornamental borders** — edges framed with decorative line patterns, knotwork, or vine illustrations
- **Calligraphic labels** — place names written in elegant serif script with varying sizes based on importance, often curved to follow coastlines or mountain ranges
- **NO digital gradients, NO photorealistic textures, NO modern fonts** — everything must appear hand-inked on parchment

## Compositional Patterns

| Content Structure | Composition | Reference |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Hierarchy/levels | Central kingdom/capital city rendered largest and most detailed, surrounding regions progressively simpler — the eye drawn to the important center | Tolkien's Map of Middle-earth with Mordor as the detailed focal point |
| Flow/process | Dotted journey path winding through terrain, with numbered waypoints at key locations — the reader follows the traveler's route | Fellowship's path from Hobbiton to Mount Doom |
| Connections | Roads and rivers linking settlements, with terrain obstacles (mountains, forests) creating natural barriers and chokepoints | Trade route maps showing connections between kingdoms |
| Cycles | Circular border containing a central map, with seasonal/temporal annotations around the edges in decorative cartouches | Medieval wheel-of-the-year calendar maps |
| Comparison | Split map showing two regions/kingdoms side by side, or before/after states of a realm, with decorative divider | Kingdom comparison in a fantasy atlas |
| Categories | Different terrain types rendered in distinct visual languages — each immediately recognizable (mountain hatching vs. forest circles vs. marsh reeds) | Legend key explaining map symbols |
| Growth/expansion | Empire boundaries shown with different line styles, oldest borders solid, newer conquests dashed, future claims dotted | Historical territory expansion maps |
| Networks | Trade routes or alliance connections as decorated lines linking settlement nodes across the map | Medieval trade network illustrations |
| Discovery | Areas at map edges fading into unexplored territory, with "Here be Dragons" style annotations and sea monsters | Age of exploration maps with blank edges |

## Typography

- Elegant serif scripts with calligraphic flourishes for major place names — Blackletter, Uncial, or Old English inspired
- Smaller italic serif for minor locations, rivers, and terrain labels
- All-caps Roman lettering for kingdom names and major titles, often curved to follow borders
- Text size directly indicates importance — capital cities in large ornate script, villages in tiny simple letters
- Labels curve to follow coastlines, rivers, and mountain ranges — never rigidly horizontal
- Decorative dropped capitals for title cartouches and legend headers
- Handwritten quality with slight irregularity — never perfectly uniform, suggesting a human cartographer's hand
- NO sans-serif, NO modern typefaces, NO digital perfection — everything evokes quill and ink

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT a modern GPS map or Google Maps style** — no clean vector lines, no uniform symbols, no digital precision. Fantasy maps are hand-drawn, irregular, and decorative
- **NOT a video game minimap or UI element** — those are functional and minimal. Fantasy maps are ornamental and detailed, meant to be studied and admired
- **NOT a political map with solid color fills** — fantasy maps use linework and texture, not flat color regions. Terrain is rendered, not color-coded
- **NOT a satellite or topographic contour map** — no elevation lines, no aerial photography aesthetics. This is medieval cartography, not modern surveying
- **NOT clean or minimal** — fantasy maps are dense with detail, filled with decorative elements, every empty space an opportunity for embellishment
- **NOT a pirate/cartoon treasure map** — while treasure maps share some DNA, true fantasy maps are more sophisticated, with consistent cartographic conventions and serious artistic craft
- This must look like it was hand-inked by a scholar cartographer in a candlelit scriptorium, not generated by mapping software
