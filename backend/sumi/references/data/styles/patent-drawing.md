# patent-drawing

Technical precision of 19th and early 20th century patent office illustrations — black ink on cream paper, meticulous cross-hatched shading, numbered callouts connected by fine leader lines, exploded views revealing internal mechanisms. Every gear, spring, and lever rendered with draftsman's exactitude. This is the visual language of Edison's lightbulb patent, the Wright Brothers' flying machine, the engineering behind everyday objects laid bare with systematic clarity.

## Color Palette

- Primary: India ink black (#1A1A1A), cream patent paper (#F8F4E8), sepia aged (#E6DCC8)
- Background: Off-white patent document (#FFFEF5), aged vellum (#F0E8D8), blueprint blue (#1E3A5F) for variant
- Accents: Red for emphasis/highlight callouts (#C0392B), yellow highlight wash (#F7DC6F) for featured components
- Text: Black ink (#000000), sepia (#5D4E37) for aged variant

## Variants

| Variant | Focus | Visual Emphasis |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **USPTO** | Official patent document style, maximum clarity | Clean black linework on white, fine parallel line shading for depth, numbered callouts (10, 12, 14, 16) with leader lines, "FIG. 1" labels, formal legend/description box |
| **Exploded View** | Components pulled apart along assembly axis | Parts floating in space along dotted assembly lines, each piece numbered, screws and small parts shown at actual size, hidden lines shown as dashed |
| **Cross-Section** | Internal mechanisms revealed by cut plane | Clean cut line with hatching on cut surfaces, internal gears and chambers exposed, section indicator arrows (A-A, B-B), different hatch patterns for different materials |
| **Blueprint** | White-on-blue technical drawing | Inverted colors — white lines on Prussian blue background, cyan highlights, fold marks, measurement annotations, engineering precision |

## Visual Elements

- Fine black ink lines of consistent weight with no variation (technical pen precision)
- Parallel line hatching (stippled shading) showing curved surfaces and depth — closer lines = darker
- Cross-hatching at different angles to indicate material types (45° for steel, 30° for brass, etc.)
- Numbered callouts (10, 12, 14, 16, 18...) using even numbers, connected by thin leader lines with arrows
- "FIG. 1", "FIG. 2", "FIG. 3" labels centered beneath each view
- Exploded views with components floating along dotted assembly axis lines
- Hidden edges shown as dashed lines (---) behind visible surfaces
- Section cut indicators: thick arrows labeled A-A showing where cross-section is taken
- Dimension lines with small perpendicular terminators and measurements
- Center lines (long-short-long dash pattern) marking axes of rotation
- Detail circles — small area magnified in circular inset labeled "DETAIL A"
- Multiple views: front elevation, side elevation, top/plan view arranged systematically
- Border lines — thick ruled border around drawing with title block in corner
- Title block containing: patent number, date, inventor name, title of invention

| Content Structure | Composition | Reference |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------|

| Flow/process | Sequential figures showing operation: FIG. 1 starting position, FIG. 2 mid-action, FIG. 3 final state | Mechanical action sequence diagrams |
| Connections | Leader lines connecting numbered callouts to components, referencing legend/description | Patent callout system with numbered parts list |
| Cycles | Circular arrangement of operational states, clockwise progression | Mechanism cycle of operation |
| Comparison | Before/after or variant A/variant B side by side with parallel numbering | Improvement patents showing old vs. new |
| Categories | Grid of related components, each in its own bounded frame with FIG. number | Parts catalog or component family |
| Assembly | Exploded view — parts floating along dotted assembly axis, sequence from left to right | Assembly instruction diagram |
| Section | Cross-section view with hatching, positioned next to exterior view, linked by section arrows A-A | Cut-away revealing internal structure |
| Detail | Main view with circular detail callout showing magnified area | "DETAIL A" circles for small features |
| Multiple angles | Orthographic projection — front, side, top views arranged in standard T-configuration | Engineering drawing layout |

## Typography

- Titles: All-caps bold sans-serif or block serif — PATENT NO. 1,234,567
- Figure labels: "FIG. 1", "FIG. 2" in consistent technical lettering, centered below each figure
- Callout numbers: Clean numerals (10, 12, 14) positioned at end of leader lines
- Legend text: Typewritten appearance — Courier, American Typewriter, or technical lettering
- Inventor name and date in formal title block: serif capitals with small caps
- All lettering perfectly horizontal or vertical — no angles except for dimension callouts
- Line weights: thin for leader lines and dimensions, medium for visible edges, thick for section cuts
- No decorative fonts, no italics except for emphasis — pure technical communication

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT artistic or expressive** — Patent drawings are purely functional. No flourishes, no stylization, no aesthetic choices beyond clarity.
- **NOT sketchy or loose** — Every line is deliberate, consistent weight, perfectly straight or smoothly curved. No hand-drawn wobble.
- **NOT colorful** — Traditional patent drawings are black and white (or blue and white for blueprint variant). Color is used only for emphasis, never decoration.
- **NOT photorealistic** — This is technical illustration, not rendering. No reflections, no textures, no photographic qualities.
- **NOT modern CAD renders** — Should look hand-drafted with pen and straight-edge, not computer generated. Hatching is hand-drawn parallel lines, not digital gradients.
- **NOT cluttered** — Despite technical density, layout is organized and systematic. Clear figure separation, logical callout placement, readable at reduced size.
- **NOT simplified icons** — Components are drawn in proportion with accurate mechanical detail, not reduced to symbolic representations.
- **NOT isometric 3D** — Traditional patents use orthographic projection (front/side/top) and exploded views, not isometric perspective. When 3D is needed, use exploded axonometric, not rendered perspective.
- This must look like a page from a 19th-century USPTO filing — precise black linework, mechanical cross-hatching, numbered callouts, FIG. labels, formal title block — not a modern technical manual or CAD printout.
