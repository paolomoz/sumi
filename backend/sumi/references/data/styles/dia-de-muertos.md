# dia-de-muertos

Joyful death dressed in marigolds and magenta — the exuberant visual language of Mexico's Day of the Dead. Grinning calavera skulls decorated with swirling floral patterns around their eye sockets, papel picado banners with intricate cut-out lace silhouettes strung across the composition, cempasuchil marigold garlands in blazing orange draping every border. This is celebration not mourning: skeletons in fancy hats dancing, bright sugar-skull faces with petal eyes and scrollwork foreheads, all rendered in a riot of hot pink, marigold orange, violet purple, and turquoise against rich dark backgrounds.

## Variants

| Variant | Focus | Visual Emphasis |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Calavera** | Sugar skull decoration, face-centered | Ornate skull faces with flower-petal eye sockets, scrollwork forehead decorations, heart-shaped nose cavities, toothy grins, each skull a unique decorated canvas |
| **Ofrenda** | Altar arrangement, layered offerings | Tiered altar structure with candles, photographs, marigold petals, favorite foods, papel picado draped above — a layered shelf composition |
| **Catrina** | Elegant skeleton figures, fashion | La Catrina and El Catrin in elaborate period clothing — big hats with flowers, lace shawls, suits — skeleton figures with personality and gesture |

## Visual Elements

- Calavera sugar skulls with elaborately decorated faces — each eye socket framed by flower petals (marigold, rose, daisy shapes), foreheads adorned with swirling vine patterns, cheekbones highlighted with small hearts or diamonds, all in vibrant contrasting colors on bone white
- Papel picado banner strips strung horizontally across the composition — tissue-paper rectangles with intricate lace-like cut-out patterns (skulls, flowers, birds, suns) showing the dark background through the negative-space cuts
- Cempasuchil marigold flowers everywhere — dense clusters of ruffled orange-gold petals, garlands draping borders, individual blooms scattered across dark backgrounds, petals forming paths
- Skeleton figures (calacas) in festive poses — not menacing but joyful, dancing, playing guitar, drinking, wearing sombreros or flower crowns, always with personality and humor
- Bold black outlines defining every shape — thick confident lines giving a folk-art woodcut quality
- Decorative borders of repeating flower-and-vine motifs — marigolds alternating with roses and small leaves in continuous garland frames
- Candle flames in warm orange-gold creating focal glow points against dark backgrounds
- Cross-hatch and dot-pattern fills inside shapes — folk-art surface decoration adding density and texture
- Heart shapes used as decorative motifs — sacred hearts with flame tops, heart-shaped nose cavities on skulls, hearts in papel picado
- Monarch butterflies as accent elements — orange and black wings scattered among flowers (symbol of returning souls)
- Dark backgrounds that make every bright color POP — never white or pale backgrounds; the darkness is essential to the contrast
- **NO scattered random elements** — every decorative motif connects to a border, garland, or compositional frame
- **NO realistic anatomical skulls** — all skulls are DECORATIVE and FESTIVE with folk-art ornamentation
- **NO morbid or gothic darkness** — this is CELEBRATION, bright and joyful despite skull imagery

## Typography

- Bold decorative display lettering for titles — thick outlined letters with interior floral or dot-pattern fills, suggesting hand-painted Mexican signage (rotulismo)
- Bone white or gold text on dark backgrounds for maximum contrast and readability
- Text placed within decorative cartouches framed by marigold garlands or papel picado border strips
- Labels can be rendered as banderole ribbon banners — curved ribbon shapes with text, decorated ends
- Day of the Dead hand-lettered quality — not mechanical fonts but expressive brush-painted or carved letterforms
- Ornamental drop capitals featuring calavera or floral motifs integrated into the first letter
- No thin, light, or minimal typography — everything is BOLD, DECORATED, and FESTIVE

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT keith-haring** — Despite bold outlines and bright colors, Dia de Muertos has FOLK-ART DENSITY and FLORAL ORNAMENTATION, not flat pop-art simplicity. NO empty flat color shapes, NO radiating movement lines
- **NOT art-nouveau** — Despite floral motifs, this is FOLK-ART with BOLD OUTLINES and FLAT FILLS, not sinuous organic flowing curves. NO metallic gradients, NO whiplash lines, NO jewel-tone subtlety
- **NOT memphis** — Despite bright multi-color palette, Dia de Muertos is CULTURALLY SPECIFIC folk art, not postmodern design. NO geometric confetti, NO squiggle patterns, NO pastel moments
- **NOT gothic/horror** — Despite skulls, this is JOYFUL CELEBRATION. NO dark menacing atmosphere, NO dripping blood, NO horror movie aesthetic, NO morbid darkness
- **NOT Halloween** — This is a MEXICAN CULTURAL TRADITION, not American Halloween. NO jack-o-lanterns, NO witch hats, NO cobwebs, NO green-and-black color schemes, NO cartoon ghosts
- **NOT generic skeleton clip-art** — Skulls must be RICHLY DECORATED sugar skulls with floral and geometric ornamentation, not plain white anatomical skulls
- **NOT minimalist** — Every surface should be DECORATED. Backgrounds should be dark but never empty. Borders, garlands, and papel picado fill the frame. **NO large empty spaces**, **NO sparse modern layouts**
- This must look like a Mexican ofrenda altar brought to life as illustration — densely decorated, blazing with marigold orange and magenta against rich dark backgrounds, every skull smiling and adorned — not a corporate infographic with skull clip-art pasted on
